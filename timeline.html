Mahabharata Timeline

Circa 5600 BCE
Birth of Pandu and Dhritarashtra
King Vichitravirya dies without heirs. Satyavati calls upon Vyasa to father children with Vichitravirya’s widows, Ambika and Ambalika. Dhritarashtra, born blind, and Pandu, born pale, are raised as princes of Hastinapura, setting the stage for the Kuru dynasty’s succession disputes.
Circa 5580 BCE
Birth of the Pandavas and Kauravas
Pandu, cursed to die if he engages in intimacy, retires to the forest. Kunti uses a boon to invoke gods, giving birth to Yudhishthira, Bhima, and Arjuna. Madri bears Nakula and Sahadeva. Meanwhile, Dhritarashtra’s wife Gandhari gives birth to 100 sons, the Kauravas, led by Duryodhana.
Circa 5570 BCE
Pandu’s Death
Pandu, overcome by desire, dies after embracing Madri, who immolates herself. Kunti returns to Hastinapura with the five Pandavas. The Pandavas and Kauravas grow up together under Bhishma’s care, but tensions arise due to Duryodhana’s jealousy and ambition.
Circa 5565 BCE
Training Under Drona
The Pandavas and Kauravas are trained by Guru Drona in warfare and archery. Arjuna excels, earning Drona’s favor and the divine bow Gandiva. Drona demands Ekalavya’s thumb as guru-dakshina to ensure Arjuna’s supremacy, highlighting the intense rivalry among the cousins.
Circa 5563 BCE
Draupadi’s Swayamvara
Arjuna wins Draupadi’s hand at her swayamvara by hitting a fish’s eye with an arrow. The Pandavas share Draupadi as their wife due to Kunti’s command. This event angers the Kauravas, particularly Duryodhana, escalating tensions between the two families.
Circa 5562 BCE
Game of Dice
Duryodhana, with Shakuni’s help, invites Yudhishthira to a rigged dice game. Yudhishthira loses his kingdom, brothers, and Draupadi. Draupadi’s humiliation in the Kaurava court, saved by Krishna’s intervention, fuels the Pandavas’ resolve for revenge.
Circa 5562–5549 BCE
Pandavas’ Exile
The Pandavas, with Draupadi, are exiled for 13 years (12 years in the forest and 1 year incognito). They face hardships, gain divine weapons, and form alliances with kings like Drupada and Virata, preparing for the inevitable war against the Kauravas.
16 Oct 5561 BCE
War Begins; Bhagavad Gita
The Kurukshetra War commences. Bhishma leads the Kauravas, causing heavy Pandava losses. Arjuna hesitates to fight, leading to Krishna’s recitation of the Bhagavad Gita, imparting spiritual wisdom and urging Arjuna to fulfill his Kshatriya duty.
25 Oct 5561 BCE
Bhishma Falls
Shikhandi, a reincarnation of Amba, faces Bhishma, who lowers his weapons due to his vow. Arjuna shoots countless arrows, felling Bhishma, who lies on a bed of arrows. The Kauravas are demoralized, and Drona takes command.
28 Oct 5561 BCE
Abhimanyu’s Death
Drona forms the Chakravyuha. Abhimanyu enters but is trapped and killed by multiple Kaurava warriors, including Drona, Karna, and Dushasana’s son, after Jayadratha blocks the Pandavas from aiding him. Arjuna vows revenge.
29 Oct 5561 BCE
Jayadratha Falls
Arjuna, enraged by Abhimanyu’s death, vows to kill Jayadratha before sunset. Krishna creates an illusion of sunset, allowing Arjuna to behead Jayadratha. The battle continues into the night, with Ghatotkacha killed by Karna’s Shakti weapon.
30 Oct 5561 BCE
Drona’s Death
Yudhishthira announces the death of an elephant named Ashwatthama, leading Drona to believe his son is dead. Drona drops his weapons, and Dhrishtadyumna beheads him, fulfilling his destiny as Drona’s slayer.
1 Nov 5561 BCE
Karna’s Death
Arjuna and Karna engage in a climactic duel. Karna’s chariot wheel gets stuck due to a curse. Urged by Krishna, Arjuna shoots Karna, killing him. The Kauravas are further demoralized, and Shalya becomes the new commander.
2 Nov 5561 BCE
Duryodhana’s Defeat; War Ends
Duryodhana hides in a lake but is found by the Pandavas. Bhima defeats him in a mace fight, breaking his thigh. Ashwatthama attacks the Pandava camp at night, killing Draupadi’s sons. The war ends with the Pandavas’ victory.
Circa 5561 BCE
Yudhishthira’s Coronation
Yudhishthira is crowned king of Hastinapura. The Pandavas restore dharma, but the war’s immense loss leaves them reflective. Krishna advises them on governance, and the Mahabharata’s teachings continue through the ages.
